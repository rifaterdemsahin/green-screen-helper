# Green Screen Shooting Guide

A good green screen effect starts with a good recording environment. Follow these steps to ensure the best results.

## Camera Setup

1.  **Manual Focus:** Avoid auto-focus. Set the focus manually on your subject to prevent the camera from refocusing on the green screen.
2.  **Manual Exposure:** Set the exposure manually. Auto-exposure can cause the brightness of the green screen to change, which will make keying difficult.
3.  **Shutter Speed:** Use a shutter speed that is double your frame rate. For example, if you are shooting at 30fps, use a shutter speed of 1/60. This will minimize motion blur.
4.  **Aperture:** Use a wider aperture (lower f-stop number) to create a shallow depth of field. This will help to blur the green screen and make it easier to key.
5.  **ISO:** Keep the ISO as low as possible to reduce noise.
6.  **White Balance:** Set the white balance manually to match your lighting.

## Lighting Setup

1.  **Even Lighting:** The most important thing is to light your green screen evenly. There should be no shadows or hot spots.
2.  **Separate Lighting:** Use separate lights for your subject and your green screen. This will give you more control over the lighting.
3.  **Backlight:** Use a backlight on your subject to create separation from the green screen. This will help to prevent "spill" (green light reflecting off the screen onto your subject).
4.  **Soft Light:** Use soft, diffused light on your green screen. This will help to create an even, consistent color.

## Green Screen Checklist

-   [ ] Green screen is smooth and free of wrinkles.
-   [ ] Green screen is evenly lit.
-   [ ] Subject is far enough from the green screen to avoid shadows and spill.
-   [ ] Camera is set to manual focus.
-   [ ] Camera is set to manual exposure.
-   [ ] Shutter speed is double the frame rate.
-   [ ] ISO is low.
-   [ ] White balance is set correctly.
-   [ ] There are no green objects on the subject.
-   [ ] There are no reflective objects on the subject.
